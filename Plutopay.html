Plutopay

Plutopay is an e-wallet application that allows for safe and easy money transfers. I produced this over five months as a course project and a way for me to sharpen my UX skills.

Research

I began my design process by identifying possible problems that I would come across, and how I would potentially solve them. I identified Plutopay’s problem statement as follows: “Our plutopay users need a way to transfer money to others because they wish to have access to money transfers without having to enter their credit card number. We will know this to be true when we see how many individuals are using our app to transfer funds.” I used competitive analysis to scope out similar products that are already on the market. I chose to analyze Venmo and Zelle, focussing on their key objectives, overall strategies, market advantages, and marketing profiles. After I conducted competitive analyses, I then conducted user surveys and interviews in order to identify which features of existing e-wallet apps are most useful to users. I found that: most users like Venmo the best, they feel secure when using the app, they use it to split costs between friends and family, and spending limits aren’t usually an issue.

User Stories

After I conducted competitive analyses, I then conducted user surveys and interviews in order to identify which features of existing e-wallet apps are most useful to users. I found that: most users like Venmo the best, they feel secure when using the app, they use it to split costs between friends and family, and spending limits aren’t usually an issue. From this research, I created user personas in order to help me understand my potential users better. I also mapped out a user journey and created a user flow for my persona in order to empathize with them more. I laid out each potential feature of my app, along with tasks, thoughts, emotions, and opportunities.

Designing & Testing

Once user flows were made, I was able to choose three main functions of my app and begin sketching. I then turned my sketches into mid-fidelity wireframes in Adobe XD so that I could conduct usability tests.

I conducted usability tests on 6 people from my network in order to assess the usability of plutopay. I observed how users interacted with the app, including their learnability, memorability, and efficiency. I used moderated, remote usability tests. I created and affinity map to organize my findings, and I found that -Users did not like the transfer button being on the profile page -The ‘add friend’ feature was unclear. -Searching for friends should not be under the current friends list. -And users should be able to get back to home through the hamburger menu. Based on these findings, I improved my design by: - Switching the hamburger menu to be on the top left side, and the profile to be on the top right. - Eliminating the profile page and creating a space for the users profile picture, username, and balance right on the home screen. - Adding the “transfer” function to be in the same spot as the “pay” and “request” functions. - Adding a “search” function to the hamburger menu where users can now search for new friends, instead of going through the friends list in their profile.
